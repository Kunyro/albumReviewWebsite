To Pimp A Butterfly

Kendrick Lamar

2015

Genre: Rap

Label: Top Dawg Entertainment

Reviewed: December 1, 2022

9.5

Reviewed by bidigin

This feels like the Finnegan's Wake of Hip Hop

It's been a very very long time since an album hit me quite like To Pimp a Butterfly has been hitting me daily for the last couple months. I remember first hearing this thinking it was nothing special at all, and now I'm here thinking just how deaf and dumb I must've been. The difference between this and other albums of its kind—the kind that leaves me winded at the end of it—is that it's not only that the musical value of the album is unparalleled, but there is a clear, palpable importance that carries the album deeper. This is what a modern classic looks like. This is the greatest album the last 30 years has given.

Like honestly, there are multiple times where this album shakes my foundation. The hollow echo that resonates through each retold exchange of "How Much a Dollar Cost" is earthshattering. The final verse of "These Walls" which lays down the whole reality of the situation, framing the first four introspective minutes into an actual context? I cried at that one and still do. There's so many moments where Kendrick took enormous risk and attained exactly the goals he wanted from them. That second verse of "u"? That's insane how he completely mixes up his vocal style, his writing style, his flow, and his diction all at one time for exactly one time in order to hit the most personal, deep core of his Self. I can't think of any other time a rapper bared his soul like that and still had it fit so well within both the album itself and the genealogy of hip hop. Splicing himself into an interview with Tupac, and having Pac interact metaphysically with his own work? Structuring an album around a single poem, having the entire narrative hinge on a single thought—leaving that thought unanswered by Pac? The muddled Butterfly concept along with the subtle personification of American capitalism, the music industry, the rap game, his own conscious, and his own sense of pride? It took me a long time to really pick up on all of that, but now the album unfolds more and more with each line and everything feels intimate and endlessly conscious. I'm honestly not sure if any album has reached the level of this one.
